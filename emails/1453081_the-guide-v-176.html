013 - Starka aka Andrew Stark - Conversation about Housing + Mix for n10.as // Real estate views nightlife as a strategy for gentrifying spaces. I want to think more critically about how spaces can be resistant to being co-opted by gentrification, by real estate interests.

A more explicit alliance with housing justice, and other forms of justice-focused work would be really great. Re-politicizing club spaces. It would be so cool if communities rooted in nightlife were equally engaged in community organizing or political organizing.

A cooperative ownership format for clubs would be really great. Redistribution of wealth within the space as a focus of the space.

Support of local over international artists.

More DIY, less institutional: community safety measures, including explicit "no calling the police" policies.

Rent control is a super important measure in reducing the commodification of housing. Building more public housing, including fully funding the public housing we already have.

The changing economics of electronic music // A band or a musician with a community will make more in a year from selling ten t-shirts or something than they will from the entire Spotify ecosystem.

How do we bring music to people physically for free, but without exploiting the artists and people that work behind the record?

These artists make the music that everyone enjoys, and the streaming services exist because of that. And if these artists cannot make a living out of it, then how are we going to carry on towards that direction? 

All parties, and especially the ones that have the most monetary influence and hold the capital, like these tech giants, need to come to a compromise, which doesn't only mean it's a completely savage money-earning mentality, right? It needs to lean towards realising the importance of the work.

The New Version Of Ishkur’s Guide To Electronic Music Has Finally Been Released // Originally created in 1999, the cult-classic website is like a genealogy of electronic music over the course of the past 80 years. It includes descriptions and audio clips from over 100 different subgenres that include well-known styles like techno as well as lesser-known niches like “Twinkle Prog”.

Honcho campout, a digital community connected over disconnecting in the woods // So in addition to being a four-day marathon of talent from a cross-section of the queer music underground, filling pockets of the dense forest with thudding kick drums and squirty 303 basslines — it was also an experiment in what happens when an Internet community takes to the woods and attempts to sign off from the forces that forged it and find itself, instead, by returning to nature.

A house-pop-dubstep hybrid known as Electronic Dance Music (EDM) went on to capture the imagination of the American public.
