Opinion: Nightlife activism needs real organization // Defending club culture means getting involved in local politics.

Many issues that affect nightlife culture—from noise laws to zoning issues—are controlled at the local level, which is good news for activists. In most places around the world, participation in municipal politics is abysmally low, which means that changing rules isn't the insurmountable task it might seem like at, say, the federal level.

As we've seen in cities like Amsterdam, Vancouver and New York, getting involved in the system really does help. The way to enact change is from within the system, not shouting at it from behind the gates. If a club in your area is threatened with closure, don't just sign an online petition—write a letter to a local politician. If zoning or noise issues are threatening venues or preventing them from opening, show up at city hall or council hearings to speak in favour of the venue. And show up multiple times. (They'll probably need the support.) Find ways to get the media or other outside groups to pay attention to your issue—public pressure outside internet bubbles is usually an effective tool, too. These are all manageable, small-scale steps that almost anyone could take.

How Gentrification Is Eroding San Francisco's Historic Leather Scene // SoMA’s gentrification has some leather fans so worried about their culture’s extinction that last spring, they formed the world’s first government-sanctioned Leather District in the neighborhood, complete with kink history monuments and art installations.

Friends of Eagle Plaza advocates for increasing surveillance and police presence in the neighborhood, promoting an “Eyes on the Plaza” strategy to avoid "unhealthy behaviors in public space," as noted on its website. Rivera supports establishing a Community Benefits District (CBD) in Western SoMa, which could fund more police officers. Like their cousins — Business Improvement Districts (BIDs) and neighborhood associations (Rivera is a member of three) — CBDs often rely on upping police numbers to protect the new developments and the lighter-skinned, wealthier residents they tend to attract.

New York state’s 2018 “Urban Noise Management” report found complaints are increasing every year in New York City, but the report’s authors didn’t bother to correlate that with another recent government report on the city’s increasing gentrification — A Blueprint for Strengthening NYC Neighborhoods. The second report notes how “new, higher-income residents take ‘symbolic ownership’ over [neighborhoods],” and “some long-time residents feel excluded and unwelcome.” One way the new residents’ take control is by complaining to the city about “quality-of-life” concerns like noise, which can eventually spell doom for spaces that have been safe havens for non-heteronormative communities.
