Music Industry Rethinks ‘Urban’ as a Genre for Black Artists // Categorizing black performers as urban is a subtle but pernicious form of racism. It groups together a range of genres -- including rap, R&B and pop -- but the main purpose seems to be to separate that music from the work of white artists.

The music industry has scrambled over the past two weeks to show its support for the black community, pledging hundreds of millions of dollars to fight racism and promising to hire more black executives. There wouldn't be much of a music industry without black artists, who helped created jazz, rock, hip-hop, R&B and just about every other popular genre.

8% of the corporate music C-suite is Black // There are 61 board members and C-Suite executives on my list. 53 of them are white, and only five of them — or 8% of the total — are Black.

I don't have an answer for what the benchmark should be, but that fact that one doesn't exist or is not being measured is in itself an issue. Improving surface-level visibility certainly isn’t the only thing necessary for systemic change. But anything less feels insufficient.

First Floor #40 – What Does "Doing Better" Actually Look Like? // The major streaming platforms have proven to be completely ineffectual. Apple Music canceled its Beats1 radio programming for the day and presented users with a custom playlist of black music. Amazon Music went as far as… pausing its social media.

Spotify rolled out a more elaborate plan, highlighted by largely meaningless gestures like new logos, on-site ads promoting black music, and the insertion of an 8-minute, 46-second track of silence into "select" playlists (which is particularly galling, given the ability to skip tracks is one of Spotify's key features).

Bandcamp, however, once again rose to the occasion, designating this year's Juneteenth (and every June 19 thereafter) as a day in which the platform would donate 100% of its share of sales to the NAACP.

There's a belief that when it comes to today's electronic music, black artists are in short supply. So this spreadsheet of black artists and labels was crowdsourced in advance of last week's Bandcamp Friday and currently has more than 1800 entries.

As quickly as things have changed in the past two weeks, upending the music industry will be a process, not an event.

The Black Square: #BlackOutTuesday and Performative Allyship in the Music Community // No act will ever be enough. One act of kindness will not undo five centuries of wrongdoing. Nor should any criticism of your social faux pas or misguided post on social media discourage you from your commitment to antiracist work either. We need to not make this about ourselves.

The work of the non-black accomplice and the white abolitionist in the fight against antiblack racism and systemic violence was never just a heartfelt Instagram post or a singular act of giving: it is a demand on ourselves and others to do better now, tomorrow and onwards.

A Guide to Being Anti-Racist In Fire Island // PJ McAteer purchased a large strip of the Pines harbor (which includes The Blue Whale, Sip & Twirl, The Pavilion, Canteen, and more) for $10mil in 2015.

He expressed his disdain for the injustices that black people have continually been suffering, reminding his followers about how LGBTQ+ liberation began with a riot, and made a call to action by saying, “We have to donate to causes that spread the word and educate.”

When someone followed up in the comments offering him specific organizations to which he could donate, his response was, “Sorry right now all funds are to the Pines. I need to make sure my businesses survive the pandemic.”

Seeing a response like that after an expression of empathy was disheartening and confusing. His message, followed by his comment, are a clear example of performative allyship, and that’s exactly what this movement does not need.

He would have been better off just posting a black square and with an empty caption. Or do like Daniel Nardicio (owner of Big Dicks Inn and promoter of the DWorld Underwear Party), who despite requests from his employees, chose to say nothing. Saying nothing when the world is burning around you, but it doesn’t directly affect you, is privilege.

In the four summers that I have come out to Fire Island, I have only ever met one black business owner.
