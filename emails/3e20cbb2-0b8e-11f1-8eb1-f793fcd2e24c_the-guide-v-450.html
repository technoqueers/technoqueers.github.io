+ BREAKING NEWS - R.I.P. Club Lambda. Located at 1031 Grand Street in E. Williamsburg, Brooklyn, Club Lambda 1st opened in May of 2022, touting itself as "Brooklyn's 1st Black-owned LGBTQ+ bar". Lambda was owned & operated by real life partners, Charles Hughes & Richard Solomon, who are also behind Lambda Lounge, an LGBTQ+ space in Harlem, which was the couple’s 1st nightclub venture. [Queer Happened Here]
+ From at least 1974 through 1975, 1051 Allerton Avenue was home to Uno’s Cafe, a Bronx-based gay bar & nightlife venue. The mid-1970s Bronx is often absent from dominant narratives of New York’s LGBTQ+ nightlife, which tend to center on Manhattan. Yet venues like Uno’s Cafe, documented in real time by the gay press–was a working Bronx bar where fashion shows were staged, DJs were booked, & queer Bronx nightlife took place. [QHH]
+ Located at 90-18 Astoria Blvd in East Elmhurst, Queens, the Fair Theatre opened on Christmas Day 1937. As moviegoing declined in the late 1960s & early ‘70s, the Fair transitioned into a grindhouse, eventually incorporating adult films alongside low-budget action & exploitation movies. By the late ‘70s & ‘80s, the Fair became known as a cruising space for gay, bisexual, & curious men. Former patrons recall a divided layout: straight films in the main auditorium & gay adult films in smaller secondary screening rooms, with social & sexual interactions occurring throughout the building. […] Today the Fair Theatre is often cited as the last continuously operating grindhouse-style cinema & gay porn theatre in NYC. [QHH]
+ Operating in the late 2000s, Hugs occupied a long, low-slung space just off Bedford Avenue’s L-train corridor at 108 N. 6th St in Brooklyn, where indie-rock bars, DIY venues, & queer parties overlapped. Though not branded as a gay bar, Hugs became a reliable site for queer-led dance nights like JD Samson + Lauren Flax’s “U.N.I.T.Y” ‘90s dance party […] By late 2009, Hugs briefly transitioned to being called The Cove, before morphing into the fully fledged self-identified gay drinkery This n’ That (TNT), highlighting 108 N. 6th Street’s continued role in Williamsburg LGBTQ+ nightlife. [QHH]

+ Interview with Josh Cheon, owner of the SF-based indy record label and publisher Dark Entries [a swimming pool library] Dark Entries has been instrumental in introducing new audiences to the late Patrick Cowley, the  producer of major hits by disco superstar Sylvester. Cowley was felled by Aids in 1982 when he was just 32. Although short, his music career was both incandescent and intensely prolific. Pitchfork describes him as one of “disco’s most important producers” – “a trailblazer: a gay man making gay music for a gay audience and their admirers” who “honed the hi-NRG style, with its staccato synth sequences that could fill the floor at gay clubs”.
