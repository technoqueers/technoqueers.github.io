+ Palestine +

+ ABADIR Has Had Enough of Futurism [First Floor] "Sometime during the past decade, you started seeing all these clubs saying. “No racism. No homophobia. No transphobia. No touching people without their consent.” All the big no’s. They started talking about safe spaces, and I don’t want to exaggerate, but people sometimes die in these same clubs and nobody cares. You also had a club like ://about blank, which claimed to be against racism, but then it famously canceled a Room 4 Resistance event over the party’s stance against Israel.

There’s a so-called ceasefire in place in Gaza, Palestine has faded from the daily news and the KKR conversation has gone almost completely silent. Yet KKR, through its ownership of Superstruct, still owns 80 festivals around the globe, and a lot of artists—including, I’d imagine, some artists you may know or be a fan of—are taking these bookings. As an Arab artist who took a very public stand against this, how does it feel to watch how things have played out?

It’s sad. There are ways to deal with the situation in a consistent way, but the problem is that the response hasn’t been consistent.

It was so weird to see the CEO of Sónar recently saying, “Boycotting culture won’t solve the problems of the Middle East.” It was so out of touch and ignorant. Even people in favor of the boycott would say, “Of course a boycott is not going to end the genocide.” There are other objectives, and they’re inspired by a model that succeeded in South Africa.

This is probably a big question, but what do you have against futurism?

If you have some sort of privilege, you can think about the future. But in the Arab region, we don’t have that luxury. We have to think more about the present moment and how to navigate it. For us, futurism is figuring out how to secure a living for the next five to ten years. Futurism has mostly come from Western academia.

I’ve been arguing against futurism for about four years now, and I also spent a lot of that time talking about dystopia. I published a very long essay last year called “The Aesthetics of Darkness in Electronic Music: Between Dystopian Lures and Futuristic Fixations.”

+ Shigella and TMVII: What You Need to Know to Prevent Emerging STIs This Summer [them] "TMVII is a fungal infection that can spread through close physical contact, including sex, and has been disproportionately reported among queer men. Sharing towels, clothing, bedding, or sex gear with someone who has TMVII can all be pathways for it to spread.  Shigella is a bacterial infection that affects the gut and can cause diarrhea, fever, and cramps. A treatment-resistant strain has recently cropped up in the U.S. among men who have sex with men. Experts say this is in part due to sex that involves the mouth and the anus, as this can be a pathway for the infection to spread."

+ History +

+ Rave Preservation Project launches directory tool for archival artefacts [The online archive of rave memorabilia features around 40,000 items, with a backlog of around 250,000 artefacts to be added] [Mixmag]

+ GAY POWER was one of the earliest & most ambitious newspapers to emerge from the explosion of LGBTQ+ activism that followed the Stonewall uprising. Launched in 1969, it proclaimed itself “New York’s 1st Homosexual Newspaper” & embraced a new, unapologetic language of liberation. The paper covered local activism, nightlife, arts, & community organizing while helping connect readers to a rapidly expanding network of LGBTQ+ organizations in NYC & beyond.  [Queer Happened Here]

+ Part restaurant, part cabaret, & part BDSM stage set, La Nouvelle Justine was one of NYC’s strangest late-1990s queer nightlife hybrids,  opened in 1997 by the team behind the drag restaurant Lucky Cheng’s. It offered dinner with fetish staff, live shows, public spanking, bondage scenes, & theme nights that blurred restaurant service, performance art, sex-club aesthetics, & queer cabaret. [QHH]

+ Fat Boy opened in 1997, notably a restaurant, lounge, & dance venue all wrapped in one, reflecting the hybrid club-restaurant model that became increasingly popular in the Meatpacking District during the decade. Historical accounts identify Fat Boy as a gathering place for Latino gay men in particular, as it became home to the weekly party Scandalo!  [QHH]
+ R.I.P. +

+ R.I.P. Ren T. Osborne (aka Ren Xtravaganza) [QHH] "Known for his razor-sharp voguing style, impeccable dance-floor etiquette, & unmistakable personality, Ren earned nicknames including “Mother Shady Elbows & Shoulders,” a reference to both his ballroom prowess & his legendary ability to command space on a crowded dance floor. He's remembered as a fierce competitor, an avid volleyball player, an uncompromising guardian of club culture, & a fixture at countless LGBTQ+ venues, parties, & dance floors."

+ R.I.P. James Barker (aka DJ Chaotic) [QHH] "Throughout the 2010s & 2020s, Barker played at many of the city's most prominent queer venues & events. He was the resident DJ for Poppers, the long-running queer dance party founded at C'mon Everybody, & was a familiar presence at numerous other venues. He was known for his warmth & willingness to support community events, fundraisers, & emerging queer artists."

+ R.I.P. Brenda Holder. [QHH] "Brenda Holder, known throughout New York's ballroom & drag communities as Brenda Milan & later Brenda Continental Milan & Brenda Continental Milan Soulja, was a beloved Guyanese-born transgender performer, ballroom participant, pageant competitor, military veteran, & community activist whose life intersected with NYC’s Black & Latinx LGBTQ+ communities for more than forty years."

+ R.I.P. William Coupon [QHH] "Photographer William Coupon’s (1952–2026) body of work occupies an important place in the visual history of queer New York. Emerging from downtown Manhattan in the late 1970s, he documented many of the people, spaces, & subcultures that shaped LGBTQ+ life during a transformative period in the city's history."

+ R.I.P. Duane Michals [QHH] "Duane Michals was one of the more influential & openly gay photographers of the 20th century, a pioneering artist whose work challenged the conventions of photography while placing desire, intimacy, identity, & mortality at the center of the medium."
