+ Queer Happened Here +

+ "Opened at 268 E. 10th St. in 1892, the Russian & Turkish Baths is one of the last surviving examples of NYC’s once-extensive bathhouse culture. Originally built to serve immigrant residents of the Lower East Side who lacked indoor plumbing, it evolved from a neighborhood hygiene service into a long-standing institution. Like many communal bathhouses, the space also became associated with NYC’s queer cruising culture."

+ "Located at 2850 Broadway near W. 111th Street in Morningside Heights, the Gold Rail Tavern was a bar & restaurant that became an early flashpoint in NYC’s post-Stonewall struggle over LGBTQ+ visibility & public accommodation. The establishment stood just steps from Columbia University & attracted a mixed clientele of neighborhood residents & students during the 1960s & 1970s."

+ "In the mid-1970s, a short-lived but colorful gay nightlife spot operated at 371 W. 46th Street in Hell’s Kitchen under at least 2 successive names: The Zipper (c. 1975) & then The Sideshow (c. 1976). Though both are little remembered today, the venues appear repeatedly in the city’s gay press during this period & formed part of the dense network of bars & clubs that animated Midtown’s LGBTQ+ nightlife scene just west of Times Square."

+ "In the early 1980s, Zippers operated at 31 Union Sq West inside the former Bank of the Metropolis building, a Beaux-Arts landmark completed in 1903 that was later converted to residences & Parsons School of Design dorms. During its brief run, Zippers was a site where music, performance, visual art, & queer culture converged, reflecting a key early-1980s shift in NYC nightlife seen at other more lasting venues like Danceteria & Palladium."

+ "In 2014, Nasty Pig, the queer-owned fashion brand founded by Frederick Kearney & David Lauterstein, opened its Chelsea flagship at 259 W. 19th St. They 1st launched Nasty Pig in 1994, designing & selling clothing from their W. 23rd Street apartment. Their early pieces drew from NYC’s queer nightlife & leather scenes: clubwear, fetish-influenced sportswear, & gear that reflected the aesthetics of bars, dance floors, & kink communities. In particular, Nasty Pig’s designs became known for unapologetically centering gay male self-expression."
