+ The Underground +

+ Trans women, techno, and the underground scene that saved them [Adobo] "Outside the club, trans women are often hyper-visible. People are constantly reading us, questioning us, defining us. On the dance floor, that relationship changes. I’m not performing womanhood for anyone’s approval — I’m just moving. The floor doesn’t erase being seen, but it transforms it. Instead of scrutiny, it becomes recognition. Instead of being watched, you’re witnessed."

+ Amelia Abraham - The Politics of a Night Out [Metal] "A UK queer club night I loved in the 2010s had to fake its own death, and then restart in secret, because too many straight people came. It no longer felt safe. It no longer felt like the party it was.

Where do you think the most interesting forms of queer community are emerging today?

The most interesting forms of queer community are the most underground (as they always have been). The ongoing fundraising auctions for trans healthcare in smaller cities, the secret queer techno festivals with 200 people outside New York, the parties in living rooms, the book clubs, the grassroots recovery meetings, the illegal queer raves happening in countries where it’s criminalised."

+ Queer Happened Here +

+ The Gallery opened in 1972 at 132 W. 22nd St, later relocating to 172 Mercer St, where it would remain until closing in 1977. Inspired by David Mancuso's Loft parties, the private-membership club was one of the most influential LGBTQ+ nightlife spaces in NYC history, & helped establish the blueprint for the modern dance club. It had a massive open dance floor, balloons hanging from the ceiling, custom lighting effects, & pioneering sound system designed by Alex Rosner. Nicky Siano became one of the 1st DJs in NYC to experiment with beatmatching, EQ mixing, & 3-turntable setups, techniques that would later shape disco & house music globally.

+ In the mid-to-late 1970s, Big Gym operated at 644 Greenwich St. in the far West Village, during a period when the area had become deeply tied to gay male nightlife, cruising, & body culture. It's remembered as a bizarre, highly theatrical space with camp décor, a chaotic atmosphere, & a clientele drawn from the nearby Christopher St. scene. The space quickly developed a reputation for sexual activity & “goings on” that blurred the line between gym, social club, & after-hours venue.

Big Gym became entangled in prolonged eviction battles & litigation involving attorney Roy Cohn. After being evicted in early 1979, Dupont allegedly launched a harassment campaign against Cohn involving prank calls, fake ads, & a self-published magazine called Now East devoted almost entirely to outing & attacking Cohn through homoerotic cartoons & accusations.
