+ History +
+ Circa the late 1960s-1971, The Year 2000 operated at 316 W. 49th St as a nightclub & discotheque that drew a largely gay male clientele. Ads from the period promoted “discotheque dancing nightly” with cocktails & music until late hours, positioning the club within the expanding network of Midtown nightlife spaces that catered to LGBTQ+ patrons during this era. In 1972, the club became Better Days, one of "NYC’s great dance-music incubators" with resident DJs Tee Scott & Bruce Forest, & a haven for Black & Brown LGBTQ+ folks. [qhh]
+ Beginning in the early 1970s, a flashing yellow light on the 2nd floor of 114 W. 14th Street signaled one of Manhattan’s most unabashedly erotic gay performance spaces. Dramatis Personae operated as a hybrid bar & 100-seat theatre where patrons could dance, watch pornographic films, & attend live male revue productions. […] Free wine was poured before curtain. The production blended explicit sex acts with satire, drag send-ups, ballet, contortion routines, & comic sketches. [qhh]
+ Located at 366 Eighth Ave btwn W. 28th & W. 29th Sts in Chelsea, Moonshadow was a short-lived but vivid node in early-1980s LGBTQ+ New York nightlife–a club that showed how quickly a room could shift audiences & how fast AIDS re-shaped the dance floor. […] Moonshadow was initially envisioned as a women-led space, but licensing delays forced the club to open 1st without liquor. By the time permits were secured, the audience had shifted toward gay male dancers & the after-hours circuit. [qhh]
+ Circa 1987-1990, Forty Worth was a downtown nightlife venue operating at 161 West Broadway, just off Worth Street in Lower Manhattan. While it’s largely absent from mainstream nightlife histories today, a handful of surviving flyers, photos, & music-tour documentation confirm it functioned as a working club space during this period, with recurring queer events & performances. [qhh]
+ Named after the highest mountain in Africa, Kilimanjaro was a short-lived but influential name in NYC nightlife history, tied to a Chelsea address that became a major LGBTQ+ dance & ballroom crossroads: 531 W. 19th Street. In late 1989, Kilimanjaro–an outpost of the long-running D.C. club–opened in Chelsea as a 15,000-square-foot venue featuring live African, Brazilian, & Caribbean music, run by Kenyan-born owner Victor Kibunya. The space was previously home to Tracks (opened 1985), a nightclub central to late-’80s/early-’90s gay dance culture. [qhh]
+ From roughly 1999 until its forced closure in November 2006, El Mirage operated at 253 E. Houston Street as a sex club & dungeon space on the Lower East Side catering to the LGBTQ+ community. It was one of the last Manhattan venues of its kind before intensified city enforcement actions shuttered it. El Mirage was a basement dungeon with a stripper pole & flexible play areas–sometimes rigidly policed as a “bar,” other times pushed beyond those boundaries by promoters & guests. […] El Mirage was one of the first instances of a womxn’s sex party staged inside a primarily male sex club in New York. [qhh]

+ How Shelter, a legendary NYC dance party, has kept going for 35 years [Gothamist] "Timmy Regisford “grew up” going to the legendary club Paradise Garage, dancing to DJ Larry Levan’s mix of disco, house, pop and more. New York house music titan Tony Humphries taught Regisford how to mix records in the early ‘80s, and by 1985 he was an assistant music director for the R&B radio station WBLS, which was known for its championing of dance music. After the Garage closed in 1987, Regisford sought to fill its void and opened his club Shelter in Tribeca in 1991."
