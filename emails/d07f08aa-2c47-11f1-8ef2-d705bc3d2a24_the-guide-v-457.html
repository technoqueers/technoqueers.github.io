+ Queer Happened Here +

+ Opened in 1904, the Hotel Astor occupied a full Times Square block at 1511 Broadway (btwn W. 44th & 45th Sts). It helped establish Times Square as both a theater & nightlife hub but also became important to gay social life thanks to the Astor Bar located inside it, which by the 1910s had become one of Manhattan’s most prominent gathering places for gay men. By WWII, it was considered one of the country’s best-known gay-friendly hotel bars.
  
+ Beginning in 1972, 1608 Second Ave (btwn East 83rd & 84th Sts) housed Plowboy, a gay bar that operated into at least the mid-1970s. Plowboy appears in multiple 1975 issues of Drummer’s “Leather Bar Scene” listings & in a Nationwide Gay Cruising Guide created by Bruce Peters. These inclusions suggest Plowboy catered to a more masculine, sexually direct crowd, aligned with the era’s growing leather & clone aesthetics.

+ Opened in August of 1984 at 116 1st Avenue & E. 7th St., Tunnel Bar was a small, ground-floor gay bar that helped establish the early East Village gay nightlife scene. It had no cover, no dance floor, & taped music instead of DJs. It was a cruisy, local spot for a primarily gay male clientele. Tunnel Bar also became the unlikely plaintiff in a federal trademark case when the much larger Chelsea nightclub The Tunnel (an important queer club at 220 Twelfth Ave) adopted the same name.

+ Opened in September 1994 inside the former Henry Miller’s Theatre at 124 W. 43rd St., Club Expo was a large-scale nightclub that quickly became one of Midtown’s most visible nightlife venues of the decade. The space was decorated with elements like monorails & holograms, based on a futuristic conception from the 1939 NY World's Fair. It was the best-known home of Cafe con Leche, an influential Sunday night party that drew a predominantly queer, Latin, & multicultural crowd.

+ Mr. Black 1st opened in 2006 at 643 Broadway in NoHo, described as a dark, subterranean, heavily queer “dance den” with parties that included Gary 49 on Fridays & Jonny McGovern’s Boys Gone Wild on Saturdays. In September 2007, however, its 1st location was raided due to “rampant drug activity”, during which approximately 32 people were arrested. It then reopened (as “Mr. Black 2”) in 2008 in the old Gypsy Tea basement space at 27 W. 24th Street. By early 2009, Mr. Black moved to 251 W. 30th, which would also close that same year.
+ RIP +

+ R.I.P. Agosto Machado [A beloved fixture of queer downtown NY, whose life spanned pre-Stonewall street culture, gay liberation, experimental theater, & the AIDS crisis. From roughly 1970-1997, he lived at 54 E. 4th St, which became a living archive–at once a salon, a shrine, & a sanctuary. As AIDS devastated his community, he transformed the space into a dense memorial environment, assembling photographs, obituaries, & personal objects honoring lost friends, including Marsha P. Johnson.]

+ R.I.P. Gina Germaine [A legendary & pioneering part of the world of trans nightlife, drag performance, & pageant & ballroom cultures that shaped queer New York in the late 20th century. She was an entertainer at venues like the Copa (at Susanne Bartsch’s iconic parties), while calling herself “New York’s Most Popular Party Girl.” She was perhaps most closely tied to Sally’s II at 252 W. 43rd St. in the Hotel Carter, one of the best-known trans & drag nightlife spaces in 1990s Times Sq.]

+ R.I.P. Bettina Köster [A German-born musician, singer, saxophonist, & composer whose career bridged West Berlin’s post-punk underground & downtown New York’s art & nightlife scenes. She first entered NYC in 1979 with the all-female band Mania D., performing at venues like A’s (330 Broome St.) & Tier 3 (225 W. Broadway)–key no wave-era spaces where punk, performance art, & queer subculture converged.]
