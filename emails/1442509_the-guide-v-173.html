HNYPOT 330: Turtle Bugg's African Art of Acid (Triple A Baybee) Mixx // This is Treezy B's African Art of Acid (Triple A baybee) Mixx, an homage to the art form that is known as acid house. As we all know House was birthed from Chicago, coming from the same gestation of 80s post disco black music that gave us Techno & Hip Hop. This period of musical innovation, propelled primarily by black youth, was yet another part in the long history of African-Americans dictating popular culture. Acid house was another rhythmic mistake, like the original blue note; an innovative use of technology beyond it's original intent by putting sum stank on it.

It was black people who used a discarded bass machine to produce futuristic & psychedelic sounds that would influence the world yet again. Yet acid house, like most forms of popular music, did not do for the black community what it's done for others. Acid muzik has been lazily copied and mass produced, with little of the soul intact. There was no Second Summer of Love in Amerikkka, no Ibiza for Negroes to spend their holiday, no beginnings of a multi-million dollar industry that could change the lives of Lady Liberty's bastard children. The Summer of 1988 for those of African blood was another year of ravaging due to Reaganomics, Thatcherism, crack cocaine, gang violence, and institutional racism on a global scale.

Still melanin keeps on providing because funk is it's own reward. And if it ain't funky then what the funk are you doin?

All songs are acid music produced by people of African Diasporac descent. All vinyl records on 1 Technics SL-1200M3D, 1 Technics SL-1200MK5 & an Ecler NUO 2.0 mixer.

Overdubs of black people ripped from youtube and mixed on Ableton Live 10 Suite Free 30 day trial.

#27 __ gentrification // Soul Summit would never be the same without Fort Greene Park. This means it stands on the same precipice facing the rest of the underground dance community, where gentrification is an existential threat coming from every angle. It's changing the crowds. it's raising the prices. It's eliminating the spaces. It's erasing the culture.

More problematic still is the prospect that parties are not just a victim in this progression, but a weapon used by real estate developers to change the character of neighborhoods. There's an excellent RA piece exploring this, with a damning quote from a landlord: "We decided to rent to bars and restaurants who would bring in the hipsters and change the neighborhood."
