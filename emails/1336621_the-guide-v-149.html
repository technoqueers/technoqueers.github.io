Business owner fights to save 'last LGBTQIA+ black club in NYC' // Over the last 18 years, the venue has become a safe haven for black LGBTQ individuals who’ve had few spaces to call their own. Now, the refuge’s future is being threatened by a combination of rent increases, taxes, business-code violation fees and other miscellaneous costs. Club Langston unofficially closed in late December and may have to close its doors permanently if Clark does not raise $73,000 by March 1.

Instead of idly hoping people would contribute to the club's GoFundMe page, Clark decided to emphasize the significance of the space by taking a vow of silence, fasting and standing in front of Club Langston for 10 days. He began the campaign on Feb. 4 and ended it on Friday morning, after standing outside the club for 24 hours straight.

At the Height of AIDS, San Francisco's Queer Nightlife Became a Refuge // From the late 1980s into the mid-90s, San Francisco’s queer party scene underwent a huge transformation. In flamboyant acts of resistance and excess, the nightlife community responded to the devastation of the AIDS crisis and intense national homophobia that followed by becoming even more visible, colorful, outspoken, and outrageous. As the disco and clone look generation drifted off the dance floor, younger people flooded bars and parties with newer sounds like techno, hip-hop, house, and industrial. They also brought new fashions, mashing up art school experiments with vintage styles and thrift store finds. In the shadow of the epidemic's height, many clubs acted not just as escapist fantasylands, but as health clinics, information centers, and places of refuge for those fleeing the harsh political climate of the rest of the country.

"Every week you would read pages of obituaries. Then, at night, you would go to the most fantastic parties," says photographer Melissa Hawkins. "That was when people really lived in the moment, because you never knew how long you had."

The resilience of Russia's dance music scene // The homophobic aura remains one of the most common stereotypes about Russia. Overall, the society is pretty conservative, with LGBT activists being attacked by neo-Nazis, Orthodox activists and other extremist groups. There are, however, active gay clubs, such as Mono Bar, Central Station, regular Sunday parties at Propaganda, or Клуб's Грань (Edge) series, to name a few in Moscow and Saint Petersburg.
