Linn Tonstad's Theology of the Dance Floor

By Geoffrey Mak, Pioneer Works

Tonstad and Nelson are now a staple in the New York rave scene. If you run into them at a party, you know you picked the right one. They participate in group chats on Signal and invite-only Discord channels, where ravers discuss which parties they’re going to, or otherwise share nightlife-related memes. At Nowadays, they are on the elusive “Friends and Family” list, which allows them and their guests to attend any party there for free. Gareth Solan, who runs Nowadays, described the couple to me as, “pure, infectious energy. Their appreciation for the finer details of dance floor dynamics is way beyond what most people are capable of.”

Negrophilia in Club Culture

By Mathys Rennela, Technomaterialism

It is pointless to expect a pathway out of Negrophilia from institutions which by design run on the exploitation of Black cultural capital. Now is the time to see dance music’s main industry actors for what they are: reactionaries whose only aim is to maintain the status quo. Black autonomy can only be achieved by gatekeeping them out of Black communities. Now is the time to burn bridges with existing institutions.

Zoë Beery Interview

By Todd Burns, Music Journalism Insider

As I started to learn about the political history of rave culture, I found that I wanted to write about music again, but only in that context. So that’s what I focus on: the crossover between electronic music and community organizing, like my stories on Crip Rave and the ravers defending the Atlanta forest.

Clubland Fixture Kevin Carpet Does Not Get Off on Getting Stepped On

By Brock Colyar, The Cut

Over the phone, Lady Bunny told me, “Cameras killed nightlife; that’s all there is to it. I remember when Drew Barrymore would shake her tits at SqueezeBox! The cameras turned a world that was permissive and kinky into a world where well-scrubbed and boring people are ready to pose for a paparazzi pic.”
