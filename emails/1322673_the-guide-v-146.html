New York City nightlife accounts for $35 billion in economic input // According to Mayor de Blasio’s office, the nightlife sector is growing at a faster rate than the rest of the local economy, while nightlife-related wages are rising 5-8% every year compared to a 3-4% bump in other industries.

While Manhattan boasts the city’s busiest nightlife scene, Brooklyn’s is the fastest growing. Staten Island is the city’s sleepiest borough and the only one that saw a decline in its number of operating nightlife venues.

MTA Memo: Cuomo's L Train Plan Will Cause Crowding 'Greater Than Anything Ever Experienced' // The draft document, created by the agency’s operations planning department dated January 22, 2019, makes the case that the planned 20-minute headways will result in “record on-board crowding” with actual wait times to board trains of “40 minutes or more.”

“In practice,” the document warns, “by drawing customers to L stations, only for them to find that for all practical purposes the L will not be available to them, net travel time impacts could be worse with one-track closure than with two-track closure.”

The new plan would result in a best case scenario of running L trains in both directions with 20 minute headways, up from the current four-minute headways on weekends. This, the planning document notes, would put enormous strain on a line with very high weekend ridership.

Meet the Designers Who Transformed Brooklyn's Coolest Club // Brooklyn nightlife is in transition. It's been years since basements and warehouses gave way to a more regulated club-centric scene. But already, a wave of beloved bars and venues, particularly of the glittery, gritty hole-in-wall variety like Spectrum to Output, have been pushed out or are hanging by a GoFundMe: victims of gentrification's rising operation costs and hungry developers.

We've lost a lot of the great Brooklyn DIY spaces in the past 5 years — Body Actualized Center, Glasslands, Steel Drums, 285 Kent and Trip House come to mind — but there's also been a shift in the nightlife scene with some of these DIY space runners opening real, legitimate establishments. Bossa Nova Civic Club spawned from 285 Kent, SPR from Happy Fun Hideaway, Elsewhere spawned from Glasslands, and these spaces are continuing to spawn other new, great spaces like Mood Ring and H0L0. There are a lot of incredible underground parties that happen every month, if you know where to find them. The range and variety of programming in all of these spaces reflect a culture built around inclusivity and progressive energy which (in my opinion) makes Bushwick the most exciting scene in the city.

THE DREAMHOUSE // While many DIY spaces have been predictably crushed in this city’s capitalist gears, the announcement of the Dreamhouse’s closing in October felt like less a fresh wound and more the passing of an era. Luckily, since the Spectrum is not like the real world, it could end with multiple bangs and no whimper. Artists who had cut their teeth and shared their sweat in the early years—like Arca, Lauren Flax, and Juliana Huxtable—gave noisy goodbyes across three raging months. Although known as a creative incubator and after-hours haunt for a certain Brooklyn avant-garde, the Spectrum has most importantly been a refuge for marginalized people seeking community, a rare embodiment of the politics of radical inclusivity, or, as xXx-cutive producer gage put it, “a larger-than-life, social piece of art.”

New film Berlin Bouncer documents lives of three legendary doormen // Directed by David Dietl, Berlin Bouncer tells the story of three of the clubbing mecca's most famous doormen. Sven Marquardt and Smiley Baldwin, best known for their work at Berghain, and Frank Künster, who worked Delicious Donuts in the '90s and King Size in the 2010s, are the documentary's subjects.

Why Varg hates techno // Techno, as he sees it, is a generation past being new and exciting. Now, in his eyes, it's a "hamster wheel" of old ideas, shaped by a conservative observance of tradition. He finds it astonishing that an art form supposedly based on freedom should have such rigid norms, and that they should be so respectfully obeyed.

Techno, though, is the genre that pays him. I'd wager Rönnberg enjoys making club music more than he likes to admit. But he describes his relationship with the techno scene, however seriously, as largely strategic. 

"Playing a techno gig in a big club, I mean, it's basically like asking your rich dad for money," he said. He put his hands together and peered up at this imaginary father. "'Daaaaaad?' You know, massaging the system for money. It's a service profession. I used to be a teacher before and I made one third of what I make as a techno DJ. I used to teach criminal kids to survive—you know, that really unimportant shit? Got paid like €1200 a month after taxes. Now I play music for people to find someone to fuck when they're high on MDMA in a club, and I get paid shitloads more. And I can even claim to be 'underground' while I'm doing it!" 

Rönnberg reserves special ire for the idea that techno is underground. 

"I mean, I've been playing in basement bands, noise shows, punk shows, black metal shows, I've been in that shit," he said. "It's underground, alright. You go to some grandma's house, smoke someone's bong made from a two-liter Coke bottle. Eat some crust punk stew that has been standing for two days. Hair in it. Dreadlock floating in it. Everybody smells like shit, looks like shit, the soundsystem is broken. Everything is horrible. Then, in the end of the day, it's like, 'Hey can we get a fee?' And they're like, 'Dude, you know, I posted you up in my house, you ate my food, smoked my weed, so, like, not really.' And I'm like, 'OK sick! Let's go to the next town, unpack our PA, do the show again, and hopefully we get paid this time!' That's underground. And it fucking sucks to be honest!
