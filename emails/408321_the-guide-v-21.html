(( Mix Of The Week & Artist Profile ))

(( Call Super ))

Call Super, a.k.a. JR Seaton, is an extraordinary producer who challenges us to rethink what we define as techno. Raised in North London but based in Berlin, Seaton has become well-known in recent years for his darkly hypnotic and otherworldly productions that resist straightforward classification, but nonetheless enthrall dance floors when placed in the right hands.

Beginning with the release of The Present Tense EP on London’s Houndstooth label in 2013, Seaton began to carve out a style that adopted techno’s general parameters but injected them with a sense of surrealism and suspended beauty. These early tracks feature warped, twisting melodies, percussion that sputters on raindrop-like, and striking organic sounds that pulse and scrape.

The following year, Seaton debuted a full-length album entitled Suzi Ecto on Houndstooth to widespread critical acclaim. Sprawling and immersive, the record careens between sensations of paranoia and bliss. Tracks like “Sulu Sekou” unfold as lush swirls of harmony, bounded by gorgeous synths, but are undercut by the plaintive cries of a distant Oboe (played by Seaton’s father).

In the EPs he has released since, both on Houndstooth and more recently on Dekmantel’s label, Call Super seems to have shifted his focus back towards the dancefloor, but in a way that evokes the same sense of otherness while remaining extremely diverse in form. Talking to Tiny Mix Tapes in a recent interview, Seaton stated, “My hope is that I’m able to occupy a very organic working place, a place where the pieces and projects I’m occupied with just bubble along untroubled with the angst of where and when they need to be presented."

Despite these idiosyncrasies, it is to techno that Seaton keeps returning: a techno defined by its diversity rather than a narrow, context- or scene-specific sound. He recently told Juno Plus: “The reason I like techno is because it’s so expansive… it’s the most inclusive genre, and it should be, in its best sense, completely outward looking and able to suck everything else in.”

This type of radical inclusion, Seaton believes, is what gives techno its power on both the individual and community level. Particularly in light of the devastating news of Fabric’s closure -- a club that operated Houndstooth and released the bulk of Call Super’s work -- Seaton’s words on dance music spaces bear repeating:

“I would perhaps add in relation to clubs that I believe that much of life is really only enjoyably defined by giving. If you go to a club, or indeed engage in any interactive situation (a conversation say), then the more you give the better it will be. In a club it is up to every person in the room to contribute something. Then together we all have something to enjoy. At a fundamental level I truly believe that many things in life are that simple.”

***

As a DJ, Call Super’s selections share the same eclecticism and depth as his productions. Left-field techno and old-school house coexist seamlessly with northern soul, african disco, and innumerable other genres that fall in-between. Moments of darkness are eclipsed by soulful elation in ways that are as unexpected as they are wholly absorbing. At all times, Call Super manages to create an experience that is at once deeply personal and profoundly moving.
