The Tragic Decline of Music Literacy (and Quality) // The results of the study revealed that timbral variety went down over time, meaning songs are becoming more homogeneous. Translation: most pop music now sounds the same. Timbral quality peaked in the 60's and has since dropped steadily with less diversity of instruments and recording techniques. Today’s pop music is largely the same with a combination of keyboard, drum machine and computer software greatly diminishing the creativity and originality.

Pitch has also decreased, with the number of chords and different melodies declining. Pitch content has also decreased, with the number of chords and different melodies declining as musicians today are less adventurous in moving from one chord or note to another, opting for well-trod paths by their predecessors.

Loudness was found to have increased by about one decibel every eight years. Music loudness has been manipulated by the use of compression. Compression boosts the volume of the quietest parts of the song so they match the loudest parts, reducing dynamic range. With everything now loud, it gives music a muddled sound, as everything has less punch and vibrancy due to compression.

Join in the chant: Inside the cult of EBM // It's clear that modern EBM music is multifaceted, and that there's a genuine buzz around it at the moment. And yet, EBM might be the most crucially misunderstood term in dance music today, so much so that it caused Adam X, a longtime advocate of the sound, to take to Facebook last year in an uncharacteristic social media rant. "99% of the music entitled [EBM] in techno has nothing to do with EBM," he wrote. Many agreed. So what exactly is EBM? Where did it come from, and what does it actually sound like?


Meet Tameka Eastman-Coburn, Dee Diggs and Cierra Peters of Evolv Tech // Given that confidence is defined as our belief in our ability to succeed at a given task, lack of confidence results in risk aversion, in this case, making women less willing or able to advance musically. Making music, learning new hardware/software, throwing a successful party, investing money in music equipment – these are all risks. We began a virtual “safer space” for solidarity, networking, venting, troubleshoot contract and pay issues, or just asking technical gear-related questions before a gig, etc. The online platform was only open to self-identified woman, non-binary and trans-folks and focused on building a stronger sense of community and connection to role models and resources.

As we grew, we heard so many stories of women experiencing both passive and blatant sexism and racism in every realm of the music sphere, as night-life patrons or performers. Overtime networks in our virtual space grew stronger and positive energy was built. Meanwhile in the “real IRL world”, the dismal ratio of men versus self-identified woman/non-binary folks booked as headliners or residents continued to speak for itself.

At the time, it felt like the music-scene was changing but it seemed like femmes were being treated as an accessory in the stronghold that the “boys club” had on the music circuit. This led us to our next evolution: booking our own events and creating our own spaces.
