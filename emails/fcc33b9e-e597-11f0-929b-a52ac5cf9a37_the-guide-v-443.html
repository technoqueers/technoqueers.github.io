+ Beginning in the late 1970s, Mr. Dreams operated as a discotheque at 63-12 Broadway in Woodside, Queens, serving an under-documented but vital outer-borough LGBTQ+ nightlife scene. Contemporary listings consistently described it as a mixed-gender venue welcoming both gay men & women–still uncommon at a time when many queer nightlife spaces were segregated by gender or subculture. [queer_happened_here]
+ Disco Sally, born Sally Lippman, became a cultural icon in the late 1970s after she started going to discos in her 70s, eventually becoming a regular at Studio 54. [discodisco_ldn]
+ Located at 114 West 45th Street in Midtown Manhattan, Turntable Disco was a gay-centric discotheque that operated in NYC circa the 1970s. It boasted “Stimulating, Vibrant, Pulsating, Blasting, Relentless, Whirling, Undulating, Throbbing, Hard-Driving Go-Go Boys!” as well as “DJ Fabulous Francis” at its helm. Fabulous Francis refers to Francis Grasso, a revolutionary DJ who invented slip-cueing and created the first seamless, beat-matched mixes. He is often referred to as a pioneering figure in 1970s New York and in addition to playing at Turntable, he spun at clubs like The Sanctuary, where he transformed DJing from simply playing songs into a performance-based, narrative experience, influencing later icons like Larry Levan. [queer_happened_here]
+ A contemporary of legendary clubs like Studio 54, Hurrah, & Ice Palace 57, Le Farfalle was a short-lived but notable late-1970s NYC discotheque located at 209 W. 48th Street in Midtown Manhattan. Listings at the time described it as a “duplex dance emporium,” placing it within the vertical, high-production nightlife spaces that defined the peak disco years. The club (sometimes billed as La Farfalle) appears to have opened in June 1978, with multiple photographic records documenting its opening night including those by Meryl Meisler & Ron Galella, who captured scenes from Le Farfalle’s debut, including its DJ booth & guests’ stylish arrivals. [queer_happened_here]
+ Jackie 60 was an underground, performance-oriented, weekly-themed party created in reaction to New York’s commercialized and elite celebrity-driven nightlife scene — epitomized by such clubs as Studio 54. Located at Bar Room 432 at 432 West 14th Street (pictured) beginning in 1991, Jackie 60 attracted a diverse crowd represented through its overt sexuality and cutting-edge fashion. It was founded by nightlife impresario Chi Chi Valenti, DJ Johnny Dynell, fashion designer Kitty Boots, and choreographer/dancer Richard Move. Performer/singer Brian Butterick (aka Hattie Hathaway) was a frequent performer, bartender, and guest producer. Butterick became a weekly producer when Move stepped down in 1996. The party continued at Jackie 60 until its final event #OnThisDate December 28, 1999. [NYC LGBT Historic Sites Project]
