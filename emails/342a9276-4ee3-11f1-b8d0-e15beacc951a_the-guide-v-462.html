+ Queer Happened Here +

+ In late 1975, Frankenstein emerged as a short-lived but visually ambitious gay leather disco at 45 Greene St. in SoHo. The club aimed to merge downtown loft culture, Fire Island disco aesthetics, & NYC’s expanding leather nightlife scene under one roof. Occupying a ground-floor loft between Grand & Broome Streets, Frankenstein featured a dining hall/lounge, mezzanine, game room with free pool tables, “relaxation room,” elaborate light show, & “what we believe to be the best sound system in NYC, commandeered by the incomparable ‘Stacy.’”

+ By at least 1977, Billy The Kid was operating at 76-07 Roosevelt Ave in Jackson Heights, part of a dense but poorly documented network of outer-borough gay bars that made Queens a major LGBTQ+ social center in the 1970s & early ‘80s. A TV ad framed the venue as an escape from Manhattan nightlife fatigue: “Wouldn’t you like to get out of the city for a while? Are you tired of the same old places? The same old faces? Wouldn’t you like a place that wasn’t too far away, but just far enough?...Take the BMT to 76th Street. We’re located directly under the El.”

+ Operating from approx. 1985–1988 at 225 W. Broadway in Tribeca, Garbo’s was a performance-oriented women’s video/dance club in lower Manhattan & an example of mid-1980s lesbian nightlife infrastructure that was both culturally generative & structurally precarious. The club itself was run by Sandy Quiros & Minnie Rivera under the banner “Women on the Move”; Rivera would later become known as one of the owners of Henrietta Hudson.

+ Active from approx. 2009-2011, Vig 27 at 119 E. 27th Street was a short-lived but notable attempt to establish visible LGBTQ+ nightlife infrastructure within Murray Hill during the early 2010s era. In a neighborhood long associated with post-college sports bars, frat-heavy nightlife, & aggressively heterosexual social culture, Vig 27 attempted to be something different: a hybrid lounge that mixed Murray Hill young professionals, gay men, straight women, downtown nightlife performers, & queer nightlife programming all under one roof.

+ R.I.P. Kiara St. James, one of the most influential Black trans activists in NYC throughout the 2010s & 2020s, helping transform legal protections, shelter access, & healthcare advocacy for trans and gender nonconforming NYers. St. James emerged from NYC’s ballroom & trans community networks of the 1990s. A longtime organizer & strategist, St. James became a key figure behind NY State’s Gender Expression Non-Discrimination Act (GENDA), finally passed in 2019. She also helped lead the repeal of NY’s “Walking While Trans” law in 2021, which had long been used by police to target transgender women of color through loitering arrests.

+ R.I.P. Pepperica Swirl, the drag persona of costume designer & performer Alex Bartlett. A major figure in NYC drag, queer nightlife, & charitable fundraising through the Imperial Court of New York (ICNY), Bartlett served as Empress XXV of ICNY, one of the city’s longest-running LGBTQ+ philanthropic orgs that raises money for queer causes through drag pageantry, performances, & events.

+ R.I.P. Rex Reed, who spent more than six decades as one of NYC’s most polarizing cultural critics, building a career that combined celebrity journalism, film criticism, queer visibility, & Manhattan social life. Throughout the ‘70s & ‘80s, Reed was a fixture of Manhattan nightlife—he was a habitue of venues like Studio 54—where he associated with the city’s overlapping worlds of film, fashion, publishing, & celebrity culture.
