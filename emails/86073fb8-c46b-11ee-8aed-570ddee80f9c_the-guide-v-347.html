Techno is everything you haven't imagined yet [Guy Weltchek, Dion TYGAPAW McKenzie, Russell E.L. Butler, McKenzie Wark, Juliana Huxtable, DJ set by WTCHCRFT]

"In regards to Berghain, I don't really care about that place. I'm probably never going to play there again anyways. I've divested from the touring economy entirely. I'm not trying to go back to Germany, I'm not trying to go back to these places. I don't give a fuck about them. I encourage you to also do so. Before I ever played, I went several times to see friends play and even though I was on the guestlist, it was such an oppressive place to be, infrastructurally. The door people are fascistic and racist,  straight up. And that's by design: if you're fearful and you enter this space, that kind of fear is going to push you to the bar, essentially. [...] The first thought in my head was: this is the place that kicked Theo Parrish off of the decks for playing J Dilla. The second thought in my head was: how am I going to deal with this crowd? [...] So many of my friends have played there, and people have come up to them and been like, "that's not how Panorama Bar is supposed to sound." [...] Tresor I feel differently about, that's a solidarity project and they've proven that over the years. [...] Berghain is a temple to consumption, fundamentally. They print money every single weekend, and don't really pay you that well. And this is supposed to be the standard bearer, you're supposed to feel so proud that you played at this place, and I felt fear, and like I couldn't be myself. And yet I played there four times in one year, which is such a strange accomplishment. It's like the people who beat you up in high school suddenly thinking you're cool and they want to spend all their time with you. [...] Everything that I care about is antithetical to that place. [...] My cheif criticism of Berghain is the main floor is the best sound in the whole venue, but it's wasted on the music that's played on those speakers."

"One of the things I've found most enraging about not just Berghain, but specifically Europe and Germany and German culture as the gatekeepers of what techno means in a European sense, is that Black artists are regularly policed for really arbitrary technical aspects that they're like "this is not techno" but someone else just got up and did a shittier version of the thing that I just did, and they're treated as if they're doing something that's technically pure. It's absurd. [...] They don't like having the sonic presence of the spectres of the past that they refuse to acknowledge. [...] And specifically with Germans and other kinds of fascistic, Nazi, whatever... As the exemplars of that, I think there's a particular desire to generate a kind of benevolent cultural production that can then be exported. And for them they hold onto techno, and when they have specifically Black people come from the states come and tear it harder than they could possibly ever imagine -- Joy! Who would have thought? -- there's this nasty lashing-out that happens. There's an insistence on not only erasing the sonic presence of Black people, or what's perceived to be the sonic presence of Black people, through policing of vocals, through policing of certain melodies, or the policing of certain samples, but then on top of that there's this nasty cop attitude that comes out where you subject Black people to musical standards that you would never subject anyone else to. Because it's not about quality, it's not about the ability to move a room to generate an energy, it's about subjecting yourself to their guilt and the possibility that their place might not be secure, and that they might just be the descendants of Nazis. [...] That desire to monopolize and own really expresses itself in policing the boundaries of what is and is not techno."
