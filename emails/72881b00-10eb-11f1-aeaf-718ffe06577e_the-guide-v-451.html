+ History +
+ The Past, Present, and Future of Dance Music in D.C. [Bandcamp] From giving birth to go-go (which you can still hear blasting out of most corner stores) to acting as the spiritual home of American hardcore thanks to Dischord Records, D.C. has played an essential (and underappreciated) role in shaping American music over the past century. […] “In the ’80s, Vernon Burch, Studio Records, and folks like that were supplying killer boogie and proto-house records for places like The Clubhouse—which was essentially D.C.’s Paradise Garage—and the roller rinks. There was a ‘90s wave of woods-raving and the classic ecstasy-fueled parties with jungle and trance DJs in the Maryland and Virginia outskirts, too.”

“There are no more underground warehouse spaces. There are still smaller clubs and bars and DIY spaces that are important for incubating parties and DJ collectives and such. However, the days of smaller collectives—like Sequence and TNX—doing big warehouse takeovers that go until 8 A.M. are definitely over. The biggest loss here to me is the fact that these spaces were so heavily Black and were responsible for the explosion of the queer underground in D.C.”
+ What the hell was the ‘cartoon rave’ phenomenon? [after ‘Charly’ hit number three on the UK singles chart in September of ‘91, it inspired dozens of producers across the rave scene to dig through their own collections of old VHS and BetaMax tapes, looking for potential sampling gems. The legal ramifications certainly hadn’t been resolved, and The Prodigy would eventually be sued over their own unlicensed cartoon copying, but nonetheless, within months, hardcore techno and breakbeat tracks were suddenly stuffed with samples from kids’ TV, adverts, and novelty records, all blasted over euphoric pianos and breakneck beats. […] One of the most notorious examples was Smart E’s ‘Sesame’s Treet’ in 1992, which repurposed the theme from Sesame Street into a full-on rave anthem that improbably went to number two in the UK.] [Far Out]
