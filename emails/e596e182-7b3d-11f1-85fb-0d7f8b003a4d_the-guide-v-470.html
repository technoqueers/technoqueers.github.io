+ Fascism +

+ S.F. police arrest 20 at 300-person SoMa block party during Pride [Mission Local] "SFPD arrested 20 people after violently breaking up the “Stud Alley” block party. They also arrested five people at the Trans March. SoMa has been the site of “Stud Alley” parties on the Saturday evenings of Pride weekend for at least the past 6 years."

+ For Trans San Franciscans, Pride Was A Police Riot [LCRW] "The Compton’s Cafeteria riot was the culmination of years of harassment and abuse by SFPD against trans people in the 1960s. Predating the Stonewall riots by three years, trans women and drag queens fought back against police violence during an arrest at the cafeteria in 1966. They shattered windows, set a newsstand on fire, and lobbed projectiles at cops."

"If you’re trans in San Francisco, you go to Dolores Park [for the] Trans March and Stud Alley, and both of them ended in state violence. It’s pretty harrowing."

+ DOJ, HHS, FTC, FDA: The Alphabet Soup of Efforts to Restrict Gender-Affirming Care [Dozens of investigations, subpoenas and lawsuits are bearing down on families of trans youth and hospitals providing the care] [them] "Hospitals in blue states like Massachusetts and New York have continued to halt care — even after judges sided with their states against the Trump administration."

+ Jeff Mills: Seeing Is Believing [What is techno? Is it a sound, style, way of life? And is it even in good health? In the first of our summer cover stories, Jeff Mills proposes a new answer to the age-old question] [RA] "I don't think it's a good idea to underestimate the raver. + Jaša Bužinel on techno’s resistance to – and resistance to deal with – AI [Quietus] "Can we hope for a new class of artists to rise against “the programmers” that shape, manipulate and control society through mass media, commercial entertainment, consumer culture and centralised power, as put in the Underground Resistance manifesto? Could techno once again become a genuine force of resistance by subverting AI technology as previous generations did with earlier technologies?"
   
+ How Brutalismus 3000 found harmony in chaos [Mixmag] "I really don’t like the hard techno scene. It's very unimaginative. It's very bro-y and very AI-y. It’s the opposite of art. It attracts people who have no passion or drive. They see a video of a DJ with hundreds of people admiring them, and then they just do it themselves, because it's not hard to do. It’s really fucking easy to DJ that genre, so it draws in bad people, and then you have mega corporations trying to profit from it. There’s not really the passion of the underground…”.

+ Turns Out, Dance Floors and Ceremonies Heal in the Same Way [DoubleBlind] "A study published in the journal Progress in Neuro-Psychopharmacology and Biological Psychiatry found that adults with histories of childhood abuse or neglect who took psychedelics with therapeutic intention at organized ceremonies, raves, or music festivals reported lasting relief from trauma symptoms, a reduction of internalized shame, and a deepened sense of connection to themselves and the world around them."

+ Kwia's last day will be July 25 [Berlin] "More than a venue, kwia became an ongoing experiment in how we connect with one another. Five years later, seeing listening bars, ambient programs, and slower forms of gathering become more common around the world makes us incredibly happy. Thank you for making kwia what it became. We will miss you." 

+ Bussy Temple: Queer Singapore’s safe space [Mixcloud] "Singapore is hyper-capitalist and very patriarchal. Protest is illegal, freedom of speech is limited. When we started, there weren’t any queer spaces for femmes or trans people. Bussy Temple was birthed out of all of this tension as a necessity. We discovered an abandoned tunnel in 2021 and started our gatherings there. Portable speakers, lights, and DJ decks. Today, we have grown into producing immersive raves in blackbox theater spaces."

+ History +

+ Celebrating Queer Pride—Especially in Times Likes These [i-D] "I looked at a collection of photos at the Leslie Lohman Museum by George Dudley who had fastidiously captured each NYC Pride from 1976 to 1981. I looked at New York Times coverage from that year and went into the Village Voice pride archive. I scrolled through the video archive Artifacts and listened to a special season all about the 1970s from Making Gay History."

"Artist and Marsha P. Johnson biographer Tourmaline pointed me to Steven Watson’s now famous 1979 Bagel Shop interview with Marsha where he wrote, “Marsha’s position on Christopher Street is double-edged. A martyr of gay liberation, she is denied entrance to many bars. Andy Warhol silkscreens of Marsha sell for $1,400 in a Christopher Street gallery while Marsha walks the sidewalk outside, broke.”
