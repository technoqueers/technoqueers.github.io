Frankie Decaiza Hutchinson on Dweller and making actual change [No Tags podcast] "Living in New York, on a personal level, I don't feel perceived, people aren't looking at me or aren't interested in looking at me. In Berlin, there's definitely a switch. You stand out, you stick out. Obvious things like that affect how you move through a place. You feel way more visible. That changes a lot. [...] We can all recount a racist incident we've had on the street, in the club, on the transport, something like that, I mean it's so common. [...] It's never prevented me from coming back, obviously, but it's tiring feeling so visible."

+ EX.700 Detroit's Blueprint: Episode 1 [DJ Cent & DJ Minx] [RA] "I wanna say around the early 90s to '95-'96, AIDS took out all my male friends. Our club was over 200-300 males, and there's only 2 living today. [...] AIDS changed the sound because a lot of your producers back then were gay. If you lose most of your gay influencers, then who's left are your heterosexual influencers. [...] Executives, billboard writers, reporters, all the way down to who played on the radio and who was the radio producer's program directors, we're talking about gay, gay, gay, gay. And so when they all started dying, other people filled those voids, and so now we're not doing house no more on the radio. This club can no longer have a gay night. It just changed the narrative."
+ EX.698 Ron Trent [RA] "Mr. Robert Williams is the owner of the warehouse, which is where house music comes from if people don't know that. It's a shortened term for The Warehouse, which is where Frankie Knuckles used to play. [...] When he got The Warehouse together, he originally wanted Larry Levan to come and be the DJ, but Larry was working on the Paradise Garage. Funny story: Mr. Williams actually used to be Larry Levan's and Frankie Knuckles' truancy officer. It's random, but it's true. That all got stopped when they found out Mr. Williams was going to the gay clubs that they were going to."

+ Together, Somehow: A talk by Luis Manuel Garcia-Mispireta [Gravity Network] "Queer nightlife spaces are  places we go to to escape the oppression of everyday life, and they really are experienced in very important ways as safer than the rest of the world. And yet, in those spaces we also still experience racism, we still experience classism, we still experience misogyny. So especially for queer and trans folks of color, for queer and trans folks who are nonwhite, queer nightlife spaces are always ambivalent, they're always full of contradictions."
+ "Glitter washing" in Tel Aviv with Niv Hadas [The Politics of Dancing] "Attending psytrance festivals is part of the initiation ceremony of how you become an Israeli citizen [this is a joke]. After the army, you go to either India or South America or Latin America, I mean, that’s how the Israeli psytrance movement started. They were soldiers affected by PTSD after the war in Lebanon [1975-1990]. And they travelled as far as they could and they found themselves in Goa listening to this combination of industrial music and techno. From there they invented the psytrance music scene."
